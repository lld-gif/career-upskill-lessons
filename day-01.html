DAY 01

Cloud Economics

Unit economics of cloud infrastructure, consumption models, and margin architecture.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the fundamental unit economics of cloud infrastructure
  • Compare consumption-based vs. committed-use pricing models
  • Analyze how hyperscalers build and sustain gross margins
  • Map the cost stack from silicon to SaaS

The Cloud Cost Stack

Cloud economics starts with understanding the full cost stack. Every dollar a customer pays for cloud services passes through multiple layers, each taking a margin cut.

Layer Breakdown

LayerExamplesTypical Margin
Silicon / HardwareCPUs, GPUs, custom ASICs, networking gear40–60%
Data Center OpsPower, cooling, physical security, landCost center
Infrastructure ServicesCompute (EC2), Storage (S3), Networking50–65%
Platform ServicesDatabases, ML services, analytics60–75%
Application Layer (SaaS)End-user software built on cloud70–85%

The higher you go in the stack, the more gross margin expands — but so does competition and customer acquisition cost.

CapEx vs. OpEx Shift

The fundamental promise of cloud is converting capital expenditure (buying servers) into operating expenditure (renting capacity). This matters because:

  • OpEx is tax-deductible immediately; CapEx depreciates over 3–5 years
  • OpEx scales with revenue, improving cash flow timing
  • CFOs prefer predictable monthly spend over lumpy capital requests
  • Engineering teams get capacity in minutes instead of months

Consumption vs. Committed Pricing

Every major cloud provider offers two fundamental pricing models, and understanding the tension between them is essential.

On-Demand (Consumption)

Pay for what you use, by the second or hour. No commitment required. This is the default and most expensive option.

  • Best for: unpredictable workloads, dev/test, spiky traffic
  • Downside: 3–5x more expensive than committed pricing for steady-state workloads

Reserved / Committed Use

Commit to 1 or 3 years of usage in exchange for 30–72% discounts. The cloud provider gets revenue predictability; you get lower unit costs.

  • Best for: production workloads, databases, baseline compute
  • Downside: stranded capacity if you over-commit; requires forecasting discipline
Key insight: The spread between on-demand and reserved pricing is where cloud providers manage earnings. High on-demand prices subsidize the discount offered on commitments, and commitments give Wall Street the recurring revenue narrative.

Savings Plans & Flex Commitments

AWS Savings Plans and GCP CUDs represent a middle ground — you commit to a dollar amount of spend per hour rather than specific instance types. This gives flexibility to change workloads while still capturing discounts.

Hyperscaler Margin Architecture

Understanding how AWS, Azure, and GCP make money helps you negotiate better and architect more cost-effectively.

Revenue Mix

Compute (virtual machines) is the largest revenue driver, but storage and networking are the highest-margin services. Data egress charges — fees for moving data out of the cloud — are particularly profitable and create significant lock-in.

The Flywheel

  1. Scale drives down unit costs (bulk hardware purchasing, efficient data centers)
  2. Lower costs enable lower prices or higher margins
  3. Lower prices attract more customers
  4. More customers drive more scale
  5. More data in the cloud increases switching costs

This flywheel is why cloud is a natural oligopoly — the economics strongly favor the top 3 providers.

Unit Economics That Matter

Cost Per vCPU-Hour

The most fundamental unit in cloud economics. Track this across instance families and over time — it trends down ~10–15% annually as hardware improves and providers compete.

Cost Per GB Stored

Storage is cheap and getting cheaper, but the real cost is in IOPS (input/output operations per second) and data retrieval. Archival storage (like S3 Glacier) can be 90% cheaper than hot storage, but retrieval takes hours.

Data Transfer Costs

Often the hidden surprise in cloud bills. Ingress is free (they want your data in), but egress is expensive (they want to keep it there). Multi-cloud and hybrid architectures amplify these costs.

Rule of thumb: For a typical SaaS application, expect compute to be 50–60% of cloud spend, storage 15–20%, data transfer 10–15%, and managed services (databases, queues, etc.) 10–20%.

Self-Check Questions

  1. Why do platform services (databases, ML) carry higher gross margins than raw compute?
  2. A company runs steady-state workloads 24/7. They're on 100% on-demand pricing. What's your recommendation and what's the expected savings range?
  3. Explain why data egress charges create cloud lock-in. How would you factor this into a multi-cloud architecture decision?
  4. If AWS's operating margin is ~30% and GCP's is ~5–10%, what does that imply about their competitive strategies?
  5. A CFO asks: "Why is our cloud bill going up faster than our revenue?" List three likely causes.
