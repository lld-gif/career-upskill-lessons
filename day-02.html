DAY 02

Gross Profit Improvement

Levers for improving gross margins in cloud and SaaS businesses.

Learning Objectives

  • Define gross profit and gross margin in the context of cloud/SaaS
  • Identify the primary levers for improving gross margins
  • Understand COGS composition for software businesses
  • Build a framework for margin improvement initiatives

Gross Profit Fundamentals

Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold (COGS). Gross Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue. For SaaS companies, gross margin is one of the most scrutinized metrics by investors because it reveals the inherent scalability of the business.

SaaS COGS Components

Unlike physical goods, software COGS is dominated by infrastructure and people costs:

Cloud hosting / infrastructure8–15%High

Data costs (APIs, feeds)1–5%Variable

The Seven Gross Margin Levers

1. Infrastructure Optimization

The single largest controllable COGS item. Tactics include:

  • Right-sizing instances (most workloads are over-provisioned by 30–50%)
  • Reserved instance / Savings Plan coverage for steady-state workloads
  • Spot/preemptible instances for fault-tolerant batch workloads
  • Auto-scaling policies tuned to actual demand patterns
  • Storage tiering — move cold data to cheaper storage classes

2. Architecture Efficiency

Application architecture directly impacts cloud costs:

  • Multi-tenancy vs. single-tenant — shared infrastructure amortizes costs across customers
  • Serverless for bursty workloads — pay only for execution time
  • Caching layers to reduce expensive database/API calls
  • CDN for static content to reduce origin compute and egress

3. Support Efficiency

Support costs scale linearly with customers unless you invest in:

  • Self-service documentation and knowledge bases
  • In-app guidance and onboarding flows
  • Tiered support models (free → premium → dedicated)
  • AI-assisted support for common queries

4. Vendor Negotiation

As you scale, negotiate better terms on third-party costs:

  • Volume discounts on cloud providers (EDP/committed spend agreements)
  • Payment processing rate negotiation (at scale, every basis point matters)
  • Alternative vendors for commodity services

5. Pricing Architecture

Pricing directly impacts gross margin when your pricing metric doesn't align with your cost metric:

  • Per-seat pricing with high compute usage = margin compression at scale
  • Usage-based pricing aligned with infrastructure costs = stable margins
  • Tiered pricing with higher margins on premium tiers
Key insight: The best SaaS companies design pricing so that customer value scales faster than their COGS. If a customer pays 2x more, it should cost you less than 2x to serve them.

6. Revenue Mix Shift

Not all revenue carries the same margin. Shifting mix toward higher-margin products improves blended gross margin:

  • Platform fees (high margin) vs. professional services (low margin)
  • Software licenses vs. managed services
  • Premium add-ons with minimal incremental COGS

7. Operational Discipline

Ongoing practices that prevent margin erosion:

  • Monthly cloud cost reviews with engineering leadership
  • Cost allocation tags on all cloud resources
  • Unit economics dashboards (cost per customer, cost per transaction)
  • Engineering KPIs that include efficiency metrics

Building a Margin Improvement Plan

A structured approach to improving gross margins:

  1. Baseline: Document current COGS by category and current gross margin
  2. Benchmark: Compare against industry peers and best-in-class
  3. Identify: Rank improvement opportunities by effort vs. impact
  4. Prioritize: Quick wins first (right-sizing, reservations), then structural changes (architecture, pricing)
  5. Track: Monthly gross margin reporting with COGS breakdown
  6. Iterate: Revisit quarterly as the business scales and cost structure shifts
Typical timeline: Quick wins (right-sizing, reservations) deliver 3–5 points of margin improvement in 1–2 months. Structural changes (architecture, pricing) deliver 5–10 points over 6–12 months.

Self-Check Questions

  1. A SaaS company has 68% gross margins. Their COGS is 40% hosting, 30% support, 15% third-party licenses, 15% other. Which lever would you pull first and why?
  2. Explain why per-seat pricing can compress margins as customers scale. What pricing model would better preserve margins?
  3. A company shifts from single-tenant to multi-tenant architecture. What gross margin improvement would you expect and over what timeline?
  4. Why do investors care more about gross margin trends than absolute levels?
  5. Your cloud bill grew 50% YoY but revenue grew 80% YoY. Is this a problem? How would you analyze it?
