DAY 04

M&A Process

End-to-end mergers and acquisitions workflow from sourcing to close.

Learning Objectives

  • Map the full M&A lifecycle from strategy through integration
  • Understand the roles of advisors, principals, and legal counsel
  • Identify key decision points and common deal-breakers
  • Recognize the differences between strategic and financial acquirers

Why Companies Acquire

Acquisitions serve different strategic purposes, and the motivation shapes every aspect of the deal:

Strategic Rationales

RationaleWhat the Buyer WantsExample
Acqui-hireTeam and talentBig tech acquiring AI research teams
TechnologyIP, product, or platformCloud provider buying a database company
Market accessCustomers, distribution, geographyUS company buying a European competitor
Revenue scaleARR growth, market sharePE roll-up strategy in fragmented markets
Vertical integrationControl of supply chainSaaS company acquiring data provider
DefensiveEliminate competitor or threatIncumbent acquiring disruptive startup
Key distinction: Strategic buyers (operating companies) pay for synergies — they can justify higher multiples because they'll integrate the asset. Financial buyers (PE/VC) pay for standalone value and financial engineering. This distinction drives valuation.

The M&A Lifecycle

Phase 1: Strategy & Sourcing (Months 1–3)

Before any deal, the acquirer defines what they're looking for:

  • Acquisition thesis: What capability gap or strategic objective does M&A solve?
  • Target criteria: Size (revenue, team), geography, technology fit, cultural alignment
  • Target identification: Proprietary research, banker introductions, conference networking, inbound from sellers
  • Initial outreach: Warm introductions are far more effective than cold approaches

Phase 2: Preliminary Evaluation (Weeks 2–6)

  • NDA execution: Both parties sign before sharing confidential information
  • CIM review: The Confidential Information Memorandum — the seller's pitch deck for the business
  • Management meeting: Initial face-to-face with target's leadership team
  • Preliminary valuation: Rough range based on public data, CIM, and comparable transactions

Phase 3: Letter of Intent (Weeks 4–8)

The LOI (or Term Sheet) is the first binding signal of intent. It typically includes:

  • Purchase price or valuation range
  • Deal structure (cash, stock, or mix)
  • Key terms (earnouts, escrow, reps & warranties)
  • Exclusivity period (typically 45–90 days — blocks the seller from shopping the deal)
  • Due diligence scope and timeline
  • Conditions to close (regulatory, financing, board approval)
Critical point: The LOI is mostly non-binding except for exclusivity and confidentiality. But it sets the anchor for all subsequent negotiations. Getting the LOI terms right is arguably the most important negotiation moment.

Phase 4: Due Diligence (Weeks 6–16)

The deep investigation phase. Due diligence streams run in parallel:

StreamFocus AreasLed By
FinancialRevenue quality, COGS, margins, working capital, projectionsFinance team + accountants
LegalContracts, IP ownership, litigation, complianceLegal counsel
TechnicalArchitecture, tech debt, security, scalabilityEngineering leadership
CommercialCustomer concentration, churn, pipeline, market positionSales/BD team
PeopleKey person risk, comp structures, culture, retentionHR + leadership
TaxStructure optimization, liabilities, creditsTax advisors

Phase 5: Definitive Agreement & Close (Weeks 12–20)

  • Purchase agreement: The binding legal document (50–200+ pages for larger deals)
  • Reps & warranties: Seller's guarantees about the state of the business
  • Indemnification: Seller's liability for breaches of reps
  • Escrow: Portion of purchase price held back (typically 10–15%) to cover post-close claims
  • Regulatory approval: HSR/antitrust clearance for deals above $119.5M (2024 threshold)
  • Simultaneous sign and close vs. deferred close depending on regulatory requirements

Phase 6: Integration (Months 1–18 Post-Close)

Where most deal value is won or lost. We'll cover this in depth on Day 14.

Common Deal Breakers

  • Customer concentration: >30% of revenue from one customer raises red flags
  • Key person risk: Business depends on 1–2 people with no succession plan
  • Revenue quality: One-time revenue disguised as recurring; aggressive recognition
  • IP issues: Unclear ownership, missing assignments, open-source license contamination
  • Hidden liabilities: Undisclosed litigation, tax exposure, contract penalties
  • Cultural mismatch: Often cited as the #1 reason acquisitions fail to deliver value

Self-Check Questions

  1. You're advising a SaaS startup being approached by a strategic acquirer. What are the top 3 things the founder should do before engaging?
  2. Explain why exclusivity periods matter and how length favors the buyer vs. seller.
  3. A target company has 45% of revenue from one customer. How would you handle this in diligence?
  4. What's the difference between a strategic acquirer paying 8x ARR and a PE firm paying 5x ARR? What drives the gap?
  5. Why do most M&A deals fail to deliver expected value? What can be done at the diligence stage to mitigate this?
