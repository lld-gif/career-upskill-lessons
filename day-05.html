DAY 05

Deal Structuring

Term sheets, valuation mechanics, earnouts, and deal architecture.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the key components of a term sheet
  • Compare valuation methodologies for tech acquisitions
  • Design earnout structures that align incentives
  • Evaluate trade-offs between cash, stock, and hybrid consideration

Valuation Methodologies

There is no single "right" valuation. Every methodology has assumptions, and sophisticated buyers use multiple approaches to triangulate.

Revenue Multiples

The dominant method for high-growth SaaS companies. Expressed as Enterprise Value / ARR or EV/Revenue.

Growth RateTypical Multiple (2024–2025)Context
<20% YoY3–6x ARRMature, profitable SaaS
20–40% YoY6–12x ARRGrowth-stage SaaS
40–80% YoY12–20x ARRHigh-growth with strong retention
>80% YoY20–40x+ ARRCategory leaders, AI/hot sectors

EBITDA Multiples

Preferred for profitable or near-profitable companies, and the standard for PE deals:

  • Small SaaS ($5–20M revenue): 8–15x EBITDA
  • Mid-market ($20–100M revenue): 15–25x EBITDA
  • Enterprise ($100M+): 20–35x EBITDA

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

Project future free cash flows and discount them to present value. Theoretically the most rigorous but highly sensitive to assumptions:

  • Revenue growth rate projections (usually 5-year model)
  • Margin expansion assumptions
  • Terminal value (often 60–80% of total DCF value — the tail wags the dog)
  • Discount rate (WACC) — typically 10–15% for tech companies
Reality check: In tech M&A, revenue multiples dominate early-stage deals. DCF and EBITDA multiples become more important as companies mature. Buyers run all three but negotiate based on the methodology that supports their preferred price.

Consideration Types

How the buyer pays is as important as how much they pay.

All Cash

  • Seller preference: Certainty of value, immediate liquidity
  • Buyer implications: Requires cash on hand or debt financing; dilution-neutral
  • When used: PE acquisitions, acqui-hires, smaller deals

All Stock

  • Seller implication: Takes on buyer's equity risk; value fluctuates until lockup expires
  • Buyer advantage: Preserves cash, aligns seller with post-acquisition performance
  • When used: Large strategic mergers, high-confidence synergy deals
  • Key risk: Buyer's stock price drops between signing and closing

Cash + Stock Mix

Most common structure for mid-to-large deals. Typical splits: 60/40 or 70/30 cash/stock. Balances certainty for the seller with alignment for the buyer.

Cash + Earnout

Bridges valuation gaps by making a portion of the price contingent on future performance.

Earnout Structures

An earnout is contingent consideration — additional payment to the seller if specified targets are met post-close.

When Earnouts Make Sense

  • Buyer and seller disagree on valuation (earnout bridges the gap)
  • Business has strong growth trajectory but limited track record
  • Key employees need financial incentive to stay post-acquisition
  • High uncertainty about customer retention or market dynamics

Common Earnout Metrics

MetricProsCons
Revenue / ARRSimple, hard to manipulateIgnores profitability
EBITDAAligns with buyer valueSubject to accounting manipulation by buyer
Customer milestonesClear, measurableMay not reflect business health
Product milestonesGood for acqui-hires / tech dealsSubjective completion criteria
Gross profitBalances growth and efficiencyModerate complexity

Earnout Best Practices

  • Keep the period short (12–24 months; longer creates fatigue and disputes)
  • Use objective, auditable metrics — avoid subjective milestones
  • Define how the acquired business will be operated (standalone vs. integrated)
  • Include acceleration clauses (if buyer changes the business materially, earnout pays out)
  • Cap the earnout at a reasonable percentage (typically 20–40% of total deal value)
Warning: Earnouts are the #1 source of post-acquisition disputes. The seller thinks the buyer sabotaged the earnout; the buyer thinks the seller gamed the metrics. Clear documentation and independent measurement reduce (but don't eliminate) conflict.

Protective Provisions

For Buyers

  • Reps & warranties insurance: Shifts indemnification risk to an insurer (increasingly standard)
  • Escrow holdback: 10–15% of purchase price held for 12–18 months for claims
  • Material adverse change (MAC) clause: Allows buyer to walk if target's business deteriorates materially before closing
  • Non-compete / non-solicit: Prevents seller's key people from competing or poaching employees

For Sellers

  • Reverse breakup fee: Buyer pays if they fail to close (typically 3–6% of deal value)
  • Specific performance: Court can force buyer to complete the deal
  • Earnout protections: Buyer must operate the business in good faith to achieve earnout targets
  • Accelerated vesting: Employee equity vests immediately on acquisition (single or double trigger)

Deal Structure Decision Framework

ScenarioRecommended StructureRationale
Acqui-hire (team focus)Cash + retention packagesMinimize complexity, retain talent
Profitable small companyAll cash, EBITDA-basedCertainty for seller, clean close
High-growth startupCash + earnout on ARRBridge valuation gap, prove trajectory
Large strategic mergerCash + stockAlign long-term interests, preserve cash
PE platform dealLeveraged cash + equity rolloverSeller retains upside, PE uses leverage

Self-Check Questions

  1. A buyer offers $50M: $35M cash at close + $15M earnout over 24 months based on EBITDA. As the seller's advisor, what concerns would you raise?
  2. Why might a seller prefer a lower all-cash offer over a higher cash + stock offer?
  3. Design an earnout structure for a cybersecurity startup being acquired by a large enterprise vendor. What metric, period, and protections would you recommend?
  4. Explain "single trigger" vs. "double trigger" acceleration of employee equity in an acquisition. When does each matter?
  5. A company has $10M ARR growing 60% YoY with -20% EBITDA margins. Which valuation methodology would the buyer prefer? The seller? Why?
