DAY 08

Channel Partnerships

Partner ecosystems, co-sell motions, and channel economics.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between partnership models and their economics
  • Design a co-sell motion with hyperscaler cloud providers
  • Build and manage a reseller/VAR channel
  • Measure partnership ROI and avoid common pitfalls

Partnership Models

Technology Partnerships

Build integrations that create mutual value. The integration is the product, not the partnership agreement.

  • Marketplace listings: AWS Marketplace, Azure Marketplace, GCP Marketplace — customers can purchase through their existing cloud commitments
  • Native integrations: Deep product integrations (Slack + Salesforce, Datadog + AWS)
  • API partnerships: Embed partner capabilities within your product
Cloud Marketplace insight: Customers can apply committed cloud spend to marketplace purchases. A $100K software deal that burns down AWS EDP commitment is much easier to approve than a net-new budget line item. This is increasingly driving enterprise software purchasing.

Reseller / VAR Channel

Partners sell your product to their customers, typically with a margin of 15–30%:

Partner TypeTypical MarginValue They Add
Value-Added Reseller (VAR)15–25%Local relationships, implementation, support
Managed Service Provider (MSP)20–35%Ongoing management, bundled services
System Integrator (SI)Services revenueComplex implementations, enterprise access
Distributor5–15%Channel aggregation, logistics, credit

Referral Partnerships

Lightest-touch model. Partners refer leads in exchange for fees (typically 10–20% of first-year revenue) or reciprocal referrals. Low investment, low control.

OEM / Embedded Partnerships

Partner embeds your technology within their product. Your brand may be invisible to the end customer. High volume, lower margin per unit, long sales cycles to establish.

Co-Sell with Hyperscalers

AWS, Azure, and GCP all have co-sell programs that can dramatically accelerate enterprise deals. But getting real co-sell engagement requires strategy.

How Co-Sell Works

  1. Register the opportunity: Submit the deal in the partner portal (ACE for AWS, Partner Center for Azure)
  2. Get matched: Cloud provider's account team validates the opportunity
  3. Joint engagement: Cloud seller introduces you to the customer or supports your pitch
  4. Transaction: Deal closes through marketplace (cloud provider gets committed spend burn-down)

What Hyperscalers Care About

  • Cloud consumption: Does your product drive more cloud usage? This is the #1 factor.
  • Customer retention: Does your product make customers stickier on the platform?
  • Deal size: Cloud sellers focus on large accounts. Small deals won't get attention.
  • Win rate: If you register deals that don't close, you'll lose co-sell priority.

Co-Sell Tiers

TierRequirementsBenefits
ListedMarketplace listing, basic integrationVisibility, self-serve transactions

Reality check: Most ISVs overestimate co-sell support they'll receive. Cloud sellers manage hundreds of partners and focus on the 5–10 that move the needle on their quota. To earn co-sell attention, you need to prove cloud consumption impact with data, not promises.

Building a Channel Program

Program Design

  1. Define partner tiers: Registered → Silver → Gold → Platinum based on certification, revenue, and customer satisfaction
  2. Set economics: Clear margin structure, deal registration protection (first to register gets the margin), MDF (market development funds)
  3. Build enablement: Partner portal, sales training, technical certification, demo environments
  4. Provide support: Dedicated partner managers for top-tier partners, shared support for lower tiers
  5. Measure and iterate: Partner sourced vs. partner influenced revenue, partner satisfaction, deal registration accuracy

Channel Conflict Management

When you sell both direct and through partners, conflict is inevitable. Clear rules prevent the worst outcomes:

  • Deal registration: First to register a qualified opportunity gets margin protection for a defined period (typically 90 days)
  • Territory rules: Clear geographic or account-based boundaries between direct and channel
  • Named accounts: Strategic accounts handled direct; mid-market and SMB through channel
  • Escalation process: Defined process for disputed deals with a neutral arbiter

Channel Economics

The Channel Math

Channel sales trade margin for reach. The math must work for both sides:

  • Your perspective: 25% channel margin on a $100K deal = $75K to you. But if the partner sources the deal, your CAC on that $75K is near zero vs. $30K+ for a direct-sourced deal.
  • Partner perspective: They need enough margin to cover their sales costs AND make a profit. If your margin is too thin, they'll sell a competitor's product.

Common Pitfalls

  • Recruiting too many partners: 10 engaged partners beat 100 inactive ones. Focus on quality.
  • Underinvesting in enablement: Partners can't sell what they don't understand. Training and demo access are table stakes.
  • Ignoring partner economics: If your partners can't make money, they'll stop selling your product.
  • Channel as afterthought: Bolt-on channel programs fail. Channel strategy must be embedded in GTM design from the start.
  • Expecting instant results: Channel programs take 12–18 months to generate meaningful pipeline. Budget accordingly.

Self-Check Questions

  1. You're a $20M ARR security company. Should you prioritize AWS Marketplace co-sell or building a VAR channel? What factors drive this decision?
  2. A reseller partner complains that your direct sales team is competing with them on the same accounts. How do you resolve this?
  3. Explain why cloud marketplace transactions are increasingly preferred by enterprise buyers. What does this mean for your pricing strategy?
  4. Design a partner tier program for a data analytics platform. What criteria define each tier and what benefits does each receive?
  5. Your channel program has 50 partners but only 5 are actively selling. Diagnose the likely causes and propose fixes.
