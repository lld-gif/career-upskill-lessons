DAY 11

VC/PE Portfolio Operations

Value creation playbooks, operating cadences, and portfolio support models.

Learning Objectives

  • Compare VC and PE value creation approaches
  • Design a portfolio support operating model
  • Understand the 100-day plan for post-investment value creation
  • Measure portfolio operations ROI

VC vs. PE Portfolio Operations

Both VC and PE firms invest in companies and work to increase their value, but the approach differs fundamentally:

DimensionVenture CapitalPrivate Equity
OwnershipMinority (10–30%)Majority/control (51–100%)
Board involvementBoard seat, advisoryBoard control, active governance
Value creation leverGrowth: revenue, product-market fitEfficiency: margins, operations, M&A
Operational involvementLight-touch, network-drivenHands-on, deploy operators
Time horizon7–10 years (fund life)3–7 years (faster turns)
Portfolio size20–40 companies per fund5–15 companies per fund
Support modelPlatform team, network, eventsOperating partners, interim execs

VC Platform Model

Modern VC firms build platform teams to support portfolio companies at scale. This is increasingly a competitive differentiator in winning deals.

Scaling the Platform

The fundamental challenge: platform teams must serve 20–40 portfolio companies with limited resources. Effective models use a tiered approach:

  • Self-serve: Knowledge base, templates, playbooks, vendor lists — available to all portfolio companies
  • Group programs: Cohort-based workshops, peer groups, office hours — efficient use of platform team time
  • 1:1 engagement: Deep support for highest-priority companies (typically post-Series A/B or in crisis)
  • Expert network: Curated list of fractional executives, consultants, and advisors for on-demand expertise
The platform paradox: Founders say they want hands-on support, but the best portfolio support is often a warm introduction made at the right moment, not a 50-page playbook. The highest-ROI platform activities are usually network access and pattern matching from having seen hundreds of companies.

PE Value Creation Playbook

The 100-Day Plan

PE firms typically execute a structured value creation plan in the first 100 days post-acquisition:

  1. Days 1–30: Assess
    • Deep operational diligence (beyond what was done pre-close)
    • Management team assessment — who stays, who's upgraded
    • Quick financial wins identification (pricing, vendor renegotiation, headcount optimization)
    • Customer and product deep dive
  2. Days 30–60: Plan
    • 3-year value creation plan with specific initiatives, owners, and milestones
    • KPI dashboard design and implementation
    • Board cadence and governance structure
    • Management incentive plan aligned with value creation targets
  3. Days 60–100: Execute
    • Launch top 3–5 value creation initiatives
    • Install any new leadership hires
    • Begin M&A pipeline development (for add-on acquisitions)
    • Establish monthly operating rhythm

Operating Partners & EIRs

Operating Partner Model

PE and growth-stage VC firms hire operating partners — experienced operators who work directly with portfolio companies:

  • Full-time operating partners: On the firm's payroll, work across 3–5 portfolio companies simultaneously
  • Senior advisors: Part-time, compensated with carry and fees, typically retired C-suite executives
  • Interim executives: Placed temporarily into portfolio company leadership roles (interim CRO, CFO, etc.)

Entrepreneur-in-Residence (EIR)

A role at the intersection of investing and operating:

  • Typically experienced founders/operators between ventures
  • Help evaluate deals, support portfolio companies, and develop investment theses
  • Often expected to launch or lead a portfolio company within 12–18 months
  • Compensated with salary + potential equity in future ventures

Portfolio Monitoring & Governance

Monthly Operating Cadence

  • Monthly reporting: Revenue, burn rate, runway, key metrics dashboard
  • Quarterly board meetings: Strategic review, financial performance, key decisions
  • Annual planning: Budget, hiring plan, strategic priorities, OKRs
  • Ad hoc: Fundraising support, crisis management, M&A opportunities

Key Metrics by Stage

StagePrimary MetricsBoard Focus
Seed / Series AEngagement, retention, PMF signals, burn rateProduct direction, team building

The Rule of 40: Revenue growth rate + profit margin should exceed 40%. A company growing 60% with -15% margins scores 45 (good). A company growing 20% with 25% margins scores 45 (also good). This metric balances growth and efficiency and is the standard for evaluating late-stage SaaS and PE-owned software companies.

Self-Check Questions

  1. You're building a VC platform team from scratch. You have budget for 3 hires. Which roles do you fill first and why?
  2. A PE firm acquires a $50M ARR SaaS company with 55% gross margins and 5% EBITDA margins. Design a 100-day value creation plan targeting 20% EBITDA within 18 months.
  3. Compare how a VC firm and PE firm would each approach a portfolio company that's growing 15% but burning cash. Different advice — why?
  4. What's the difference between an operating partner and a board member? When would you deploy each for a struggling portfolio company?
  5. A portfolio company founder says "Your platform team's advice isn't relevant to our market." How do you handle this and still add value?
