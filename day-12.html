DAY 12

VC Network & Operating Partner Playbook

Building LP relationships, deal flow networks, and platform services.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the LP-GP relationship and fundraising dynamics
  • Build and maintain a deal flow network
  • Design platform services that differentiate a fund
  • Navigate the path from operator to investor

The LP-GP Relationship

Venture capital funds are structured as limited partnerships. General Partners (GPs) manage the fund and make investment decisions. Limited Partners (LPs) provide the capital.

LP Types

LP TypeTypical Allocation to VCDecision Process
University endowments10–25% of portfolioInvestment committee, 3–6 month cycle
Pension funds5–15%Board approval, formal RFP process
Family offices5–30%Principal-driven, fast decisions possible
Fund of funds100% (their mandate)Dedicated VC allocation team
Sovereign wealth funds3–10%Institutional process, often co-invest
Corporate LPsStrategic allocationStrategic fit + financial return

What LPs Evaluate

  • Track record: DPI (distributions to paid-in capital), TVPI, IRR across prior funds
  • Team: Stability, complementary skills, succession planning
  • Strategy differentiation: What's the unique angle? Sector focus, stage, geography, network
  • Portfolio construction: Number of investments, reserve ratios, follow-on strategy
  • Fund terms: Management fee (typically 2%), carry (typically 20%), fund size, investment period
The fundraising reality: First-time funds are the hardest to raise. LPs default to re-upping with existing managers. Breaking through requires either an exceptional track record as an angel/operator, a differentiated strategy LPs can't access elsewhere, or strong personal relationships with LP allocators.

Fund Economics

Understanding how GPs make money clarifies incentive alignment:

  • Management fee: 2% of committed capital annually. On a $200M fund, that's $4M/year to run the firm. Covers salaries, rent, travel, platform team.
  • Carried interest: 20% of profits above the hurdle rate (usually 8%). This is where the real wealth creation happens for GPs.
  • GP commitment: GPs typically invest 1–5% of the fund with their own money. Ensures skin in the game.

Building a Deal Flow Network

The quality of a VC's deal flow determines fund returns more than any other factor. Great deal flow means seeing the best companies early and winning competitive rounds.

Deal Flow Sources

SourceQualityHow to Build
Founder referralsHighestBe genuinely helpful to portfolio founders — they refer their best friends
Co-investor networkHighBuild trust with other VCs through co-investing and sharing deal flow
Accelerators / incubatorsMedium-HighMentor at YC, Techstars, etc. — see companies early
Thesis-driven outboundVariablePublish investment theses, reach companies building in your areas
Inbound (website, brand)Low-MediumContent, social presence, conference speaking
Bankers / advisorsMediumLater-stage, competitive processes — less proprietary

Network Building Tactics

  • Content as deal flow: Write about your investment thesis. Founders self-select when they see you understand their space.
  • Community building: Host dinners, Slack groups, or events for founders in your focus areas
  • Scout programs: Pay founders, operators, and experts to refer deals. Typically $1–5K per qualified introduction.
  • University relationships: Teach, mentor, judge pitch competitions at top CS/MBA programs
  • Open source / developer communities: For dev-tool focused funds, being active in developer communities is essential deal flow
The 80/20 of deal flow: 80% of a fund's returns typically come from deals sourced through the GP's personal network — not inbound applications. Invest heavily in relationships with the 50–100 people who consistently see the best companies: other GPs, prolific angels, top accelerator partners, and successful repeat founders.

Platform Services as Competitive Advantage

In competitive rounds, platform services can tip a founder's decision. The best platforms go beyond generic support:

High-Impact Platform Services

  1. Customer introductions: The single most valuable service. Connect portfolio companies to potential customers in your LP and co-investor network.
  2. Executive recruiting: Help fill VP+ roles. Maintain a network of executives who want to join venture-backed companies.
  3. Fundraising support: Warm introductions to later-stage investors. Prep for board meetings and due diligence.
  4. M&A advisory: When portfolio companies are acquisition targets or acquirers, provide guidance and introductions.
  5. Technical advisory: Architecture reviews, security audits, infrastructure optimization — especially valuable for early-stage companies.

The Operator-to-Investor Pathway

Transitioning from operating to investing is one of the most common career paths into VC/PE. Understanding the pathway helps you position for it.

Common Entry Points

  • Venture Partner / Advisor: Part-time role evaluating deals in your domain expertise. Paid per deal or with small carry allocation.
  • EIR (Entrepreneur-in-Residence): 6–18 month residency at a fund. Evaluate deals, support portfolio, develop thesis. May lead to GP track or launching a portfolio company.
  • Operating Partner: Full-time operational support role. Work with 3–5 portfolio companies. Compensated with salary + carry.
  • GP / Partner: Full investment decision-making authority. Requires either fund-raising ability, exceptional deal sourcing, or both.

What Funds Look For in Operators

  • Domain expertise: Deep knowledge in a specific vertical (security, infrastructure, fintech, defense)
  • Network: Relationships with founders, executives, and potential customers in your domain
  • Pattern recognition: Ability to evaluate businesses quickly based on operational experience
  • Board readiness: Can you be a productive, helpful board member from day one?
  • Deal sourcing ability: Can you bring proprietary deal flow the fund wouldn't otherwise see?
The credibility stack: Successful exits as a founder > C-suite at a successful startup > VP-level at a scaled company > early employee at a breakout company. Each level gives you different credibility with different founders. The most effective investor-operators match their credibility with the stage they invest in.

Self-Check Questions

  1. You're raising a first-time $50M fund focused on defense tech. Which LP types would you target and why? What's your pitch differentiation?
  2. Design a scout program for a seed-stage VC fund. Who are your scouts, how do you compensate them, and what deal flow do you expect?
  3. A portfolio founder says your competitor fund offered them a term sheet with a lower valuation but better platform services. How do you compete?
  4. You're an operating executive considering an EIR role at a growth equity fund. What should you negotiate for in the arrangement? What are the risks?
  5. Explain why DPI matters more than IRR to LPs evaluating a fund for re-up. When would IRR be more relevant?
