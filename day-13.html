DAY 13

Executive Presence & Board Communication

Board deck structure, executive storytelling, and stakeholder management.

Learning Objectives

  • Structure a board deck that drives decisions, not just reports status
  • Communicate with executives using their language and priorities
  • Manage up effectively in high-stakes environments
  • Build the habits that create lasting executive presence

Board Deck Structure

A board meeting is not a status report. It's a decision-making session. Every slide should either inform a decision or provide context required for one.

The Standard Board Deck

  1. Executive Summary (1 slide): State of the business in 3–5 bullet points. What's working, what's not, what you need from the board. This slide is the most important — many board members form their opinion here.
  2. Key Metrics Dashboard (1–2 slides): ARR, growth rate, burn, runway, NRR, pipeline, headcount. Show trailing 6–12 months with trend lines. Highlight metrics that are off-plan in red.
  3. Financial Summary (1–2 slides): P&L actuals vs. budget. Cash position and runway. Key variances with brief explanation.
  4. Go-to-Market Update (2–3 slides): Pipeline, wins/losses, sales efficiency, marketing metrics. Include specific deal narratives for large opportunities.
  5. Product Update (1–2 slides): What shipped, what's next, customer feedback. Keep this concise — boards care about outcomes, not feature lists.
  6. Team & Organization (1 slide): Key hires, departures, org changes, culture health. Only include if there are material changes.
  7. Strategic Discussion (2–3 slides): The meat of the meeting. Frame 1–2 strategic questions for board input. Provide options with trade-offs, not just problems.
  8. Asks (1 slide): What specific actions, approvals, or introductions do you need from the board?
The pre-read rule: Send the full deck 48–72 hours before the meeting. Board members who've read the deck can skip the status update and spend meeting time on strategic discussion. If you don't send a pre-read, you'll spend 60% of the meeting on updates and 40% on what matters.

Board Deck Anti-Patterns

  • The novel: 50+ slides that no one reads. Keep it to 15–20 slides with an appendix.
  • All green: Everything looks great — board members know it's not true and lose trust. Show problems with proposed solutions.
  • Vanity metrics: Total signups, page views, press mentions. Boards want revenue, retention, and unit economics.
  • No asks: If you don't ask the board for anything, they wonder why they're there.
  • Surprise bad news: Never surprise the board in a meeting. Call board members individually before the meeting if there's material bad news.

Executive Communication

The Pyramid Principle

Executive communication follows the pyramid principle: lead with the conclusion, then provide supporting evidence. This is the opposite of how most people naturally communicate (build-up → conclusion).

  • Level 1 (lead): The answer, recommendation, or key message
  • Level 2 (support): 2–3 supporting arguments or data points
  • Level 3 (detail): Evidence and specifics (only if asked)

Example: Communicating a Budget Overrun

ApproachMessage
Bad (bottom-up)"So we had some issues with the vendor contract, and then the team grew faster than planned, and hosting costs went up because of the new feature, and..."
Good (pyramid)"We're $200K over budget for Q1. Two drivers: unplanned infrastructure scaling for the enterprise launch (+$130K) and accelerated hiring to fill critical security roles (+$70K). I recommend we absorb the infrastructure cost from Q2 reserves and slow hiring in Q2 to compensate. Here's the revised forecast."

Framing Decisions

When presenting decisions to executives, use this structure:

  1. Context: Why this decision matters now (1–2 sentences)
  2. Options: 2–3 viable options with trade-offs (not strawmen)
  3. Recommendation: Your recommended option and why
  4. Ask: What you need from the audience (approval, input, resources)

Managing Up

  • No surprises: Flag issues early, even before you have solutions. Executives hate learning about problems late.
  • Bring solutions: When raising a problem, always have at least one proposed path forward.
  • Know their priorities: Frame everything in terms of what your executive cares about (revenue, risk, timeline, customer impact).
  • Calibrate detail level: CEOs want the headline. VPs want the strategy. Directors want the plan. Match your communication to your audience.
  • Written > verbal for complex topics: Send a brief written summary before requesting a meeting. Executives can process written content faster than live presentations.
The trust equation: Executive trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) ÷ Self-Orientation. Being right (credibility) matters less than doing what you said you'd do (reliability). And both matter less than demonstrating you're optimizing for the company, not your own career (low self-orientation).

Executive Presence

Executive presence isn't a personality trait — it's a set of learnable behaviors. The core components:

Gravitas

  • Speak with conviction: State your position clearly. Hedging undermines credibility. "I recommend X because Y" beats "Maybe we could potentially consider X."
  • Stay composed under pressure: When challenged, pause, acknowledge the point, and respond deliberately. Reacting emotionally destroys presence.
  • Show intellectual honesty: Say "I don't know, but I'll find out" rather than bluffing. Admitting gaps earns more respect than faking knowledge.
  • Make decisions: Executives who defer everything lack presence. Take a position, even if imperfect.

Communication

  • Be concise: The ability to distill complex topics into clear, brief statements is the hallmark of executive communication.
  • Use data: Quantify your points. "Customer satisfaction improved" vs. "NPS went from 32 to 58 in two quarters."
  • Tell stories: Frame data within narratives. A customer story that illustrates a trend is more memorable than the trend alone.
  • Read the room: Adjust pace, depth, and energy based on your audience's engagement. If eyes are glazing, you've lost them — change approach.

Appearance & Energy

  • Match the context: Slightly overdressed beats underdressed in ambiguous situations.
  • Physical presence: Eye contact, posture, controlled gestures, appropriate pace of speech.
  • Energy management: High-stakes meetings require peak energy. Schedule preparation time before important presentations.

Difficult Conversations

Delivering Bad News to Boards

  1. Call each board member individually before the meeting
  2. State the problem clearly and directly — don't bury it
  3. Own the accountability — don't blame external factors or team members
  4. Present your plan to address it with timeline and milestones
  5. Ask for specific help or input

Disagreeing with Senior Leaders

  • Acknowledge their position: "I understand the reasoning behind X approach..."
  • Present data: "The data shows Y, which suggests a different conclusion..."
  • Propose an alternative: "I'd recommend Z because [specific reasons]..."
  • Commit to the decision: Once a decision is made, execute fully — even if it's not your preferred option. Undermining decisions post-meeting destroys trust.

Self-Check Questions

  1. Your company missed its quarterly revenue target by 15%. Draft the executive summary slide for the board meeting in 3 bullet points.
  2. You need to convince the CEO to invest $500K in a new product line. Structure your pitch using the pyramid principle.
  3. A board member consistently derails meetings with tangential questions. How do you manage this without alienating them?
  4. You disagree with your manager's strategic direction for the department. Walk through how you'd raise this and what you'd do if they disagree with your position.
  5. What's the difference between executive presence and confidence? Can someone be confident but lack executive presence?
