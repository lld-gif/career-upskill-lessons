Building Your 90-Day Plan

Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1–30)

  • Pick your spike: Choose the one domain where you'll invest the most development energy
  • Identify 3 skill gaps: Based on your self-assessment, pick the 3 most impactful gaps to close
  • Build your reading list: 2–3 books or deep resources per gap area
  • Find mentors: Identify 2–3 people who are level 4–5 in your target areas. Reach out for advice.
  • Set up learning systems: Weekly newsletter subscriptions, podcast rotations, community memberships

Phase 2: Practice (Days 31–60)

  • Apply at work: Find a project or initiative where you can practice your target skills
  • Teach others: Write a memo, give a presentation, or lead a discussion on what you're learning. Teaching accelerates mastery.
  • Build your network: Attend 2–3 events (virtual or in-person) in your target domain. Have specific goals for each.
  • Get feedback: Ask your mentors to evaluate your progress. Adjust your plan based on their input.

Phase 3: Positioning (Days 61–90)

  • Update your narrative: Revise your LinkedIn, resume, and personal pitch to reflect your expanded capabilities
  • Create artifacts: Write an article, build a model, or create a framework that demonstrates your new knowledge
  • Expand your network: Leverage your new knowledge to engage with people you couldn't have had meaningful conversations with 90 days ago
  • Plan the next 90 days: Reassess and set new goals based on what you've learned

The 5-Hour Rule

Dedicate at least 5 hours per week to deliberate learning. This isn't reading Hacker News — it's focused, structured learning with clear objectives. The compounding effect over 12 months is enormous.

Learning compounds: An operator who invests 5 hours/week in structured learning for 12 months will have ~250 hours of deliberate development. That's the equivalent of a semester of graduate study, focused entirely on topics that matter for your career. Most people do zero hours of deliberate learning after school. This is your edge.

The Career Leverage Formula

Career leverage comes from combining skills that are individually common but rarely found together:

High-Leverage Combinations

  • Technical depth + commercial acumen: Engineers who understand unit economics and can sell are extraordinarily rare and valuable
  • Sales execution + financial modeling: Sales leaders who can build business cases and speak CFO language close larger deals
  • Defense domain + tech fluency: People who understand both procurement and modern software architecture bridge a massive gap
  • Operating experience + investment judgment: Operators who can evaluate businesses and advise boards command premium roles
  • Executive communication + deep analysis: People who can both do the work and present it to boards are rare at every level

The Career Moat

Like business moats, career moats protect your competitive position. The strongest career moats come from:

  1. Rare skill combinations: Stacking two or three domains that rarely overlap
  2. Relationship networks: Trust-based connections that can't be replicated or automated
  3. Track record: Documented results that create credibility for future opportunities
  4. Domain expertise: Deep knowledge in a specific vertical that takes years to build
  5. Clearance + credentials: In defense/government, clearances and certifications that create literal barriers to entry

Final Reflection

Over 14 days, you've covered ground that spans cloud infrastructure to board rooms, from enterprise sales methodology to defense procurement, from deal structuring to portfolio operations. No one masters all of this — the goal is to know enough across domains to be dangerous and to go deep where it matters most for your trajectory.

The operators who compound fastest share three traits: they learn deliberately, they apply immediately, and they teach others. Do all three and the gap between you and your peers widens every quarter.

Your one action: Before you close this page, write down the single most important thing you learned across all 14 days and one specific action you'll take this week to apply it. Not three things. One thing. Specificity beats ambition.
